Respiratory Failure & Mechanical Ventilation

‚ñº

Core Concepts to Master

Types of Respiratory Failure

  • Type I (Hypoxemic): PaO2 <60 mmHg on room air
    • A-a gradient elevated
    • Causes: pneumonia, ARDS, pulmonary edema, PE
  • Type II (Hypercapnic): PaCO2 >50 mmHg
    • A-a gradient normal or elevated
    • Causes: COPD, neuromuscular disease, CNS depression, chest wall disorders

Indications for Intubation

  • Airway protection (GCS <8)
  • Hypoxemia refractory to NIV/high-flow O2
  • Severe hypercapnia with altered mental status
  • Impending respiratory arrest
  • Need for procedural sedation

Mechanical Ventilation Basics

Initial Ventilator Settings

  • Mode: Volume control (AC) vs. Pressure control
  • Tidal volume: 6-8 mL/kg ideal body weight (ARDS: 6 mL/kg)
  • Respiratory rate: 12-16 (adjust for pH)
  • FiO2: Start 100%, then titrate to SpO2 88-95%
  • PEEP: Start 5 cm H2O, titrate based on oxygenation
Ideal Body Weight (IBW) Calculation
Males: 50 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 feet
Females: 45.5 kg + 2.3 kg per inch over 5 feet

Ventilator Modes

  • AC (Assist Control): Set rate + tidal volume, patient can trigger additional breaths
  • SIMV: Set breaths + spontaneous breathing
  • Pressure Support: Patient-triggered, pressure-targeted
  • APRV: Two levels of CPAP, used in severe ARDS

Key Ventilator Parameters

  • Plateau pressure (Pplat): Should be <30 cm H2O (lung-protective)
  • Driving pressure (Pplat - PEEP): Should be <15 cm H2O
  • Static compliance: Tidal volume / (Pplat - PEEP)

ARDS Management

Berlin Definition

  • Timing: Within 1 week of known insult
  • Imaging: Bilateral opacities not explained by effusions/nodules
  • Origin: Not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload
  • Oxygenation (on PEEP ‚â•5):
    • Mild: PaO2/FiO2 200-300
    • Moderate: PaO2/FiO2 100-200
    • Severe: PaO2/FiO2 <100

Evidence-Based ARDS Management

  1. Lung-protective ventilation ARMA
    • Tidal volume: 6 mL/kg IBW
    • Plateau pressure: <30 cm H2O
  2. Higher PEEP ALVEOLI LOVS
    • Use PEEP/FiO2 tables
    • Consider recruitment maneuvers
  3. Prone positioning PROSEVA
    • For PaO2/FiO2 <150
    • 16+ hours per day
    • 50% mortality reduction
  4. Neuromuscular blockade ACURASYS
    • For PaO2/FiO2 <150 in first 48 hours
    • Cisatracurium infusion for 48 hours
    • Re-evaluated by ROSE trial (less clear benefit with lighter sedation)
  5. Conservative fluid strategy FACTT
    • After initial resuscitation
    • Target CVP <4 or negative fluid balance
  6. Consider ECMO for refractory hypoxemia
    • PaO2/FiO2 <80 or pH <7.25 with PaCO2 >60

Ventilator Liberation

Daily Assessment for SBT Readiness

  • Adequate oxygenation (FiO2 ‚â§50%, PEEP ‚â§8)
  • Hemodynamic stability (no/minimal vasopressors)
  • Able to initiate inspiratory effort
  • No planned procedures requiring sedation

Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)

  • Method: T-piece or PS 5-7 cm H2O for 30-120 minutes
  • Pass criteria:
    • RR <35 breaths/min
    • SpO2 >90%
    • HR <140 or <20% change
    • SBP 90-180 mmHg
    • No distress/diaphoresis
Rapid Shallow Breathing Index (RSBI)
RSBI = RR / Tidal volume (in liters)
<105 predicts successful extubation

Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV)

Strong Indications

  • COPD exacerbations with respiratory acidosis (pH 7.25-7.35)
  • Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
  • Immunocompromised patients with respiratory failure
  • Post-extubation respiratory failure (in select patients)

Contraindications

  • Inability to protect airway
  • Hemodynamic instability
  • Facial trauma/burns
  • Recent upper GI surgery
  • Copious secretions/emesis

Settings

  • IPAP: Start 8-10, titrate to 12-20 cm H2O
  • EPAP: Start 4-5 cm H2O
  • Monitor for 1-2 hours: if no improvement ‚Üí intubate

High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)

  • Flow rates up to 60 L/min
  • FiO2 up to 100%
  • Generates low-level PEEP (3-5 cm H2O)
  • Washes out dead space

FLORALI trial: Reduced intubation in hypoxemic respiratory failure vs. standard O2

Shock & Hemodynamics

‚ñº

Four Types of Shock

1. Distributive Shock

  • Causes: Sepsis, anaphylaxis, neurogenic
  • Findings: Warm extremities, wide pulse pressure, low SVR
  • Treatment: Fluids ‚Üí vasopressors (norepinephrine)

2. Cardiogenic Shock

  • Causes: MI, acute valvular disease, myocarditis, post-cardiac arrest
  • Findings: Cold/clammy, elevated JVP, pulmonary edema
  • Markers: Cardiac index <2.2 L/min/m¬≤, PCWP >18 mmHg
  • Treatment: Identify cause, inotropes (dobutamine), mechanical support (IABP, Impella)

3. Hypovolemic Shock

  • Causes: Hemorrhage, GI losses, burns, third-spacing
  • Findings: Flat JVP, dry mucous membranes, oliguria
  • Treatment: Fluid resuscitation, blood products if hemorrhagic

4. Obstructive Shock

  • Causes: PE, tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, massive RV infarction
  • Findings: Elevated JVP, hypotension, narrow pulse pressure
  • Treatment: Relieve obstruction

Hemodynamic Monitoring

Laboratory Markers

  • Lactate: >2 suggests tissue hypoperfusion (but many causes)
  • ScvO2 (central venous O2 saturation):
    • Normal 70-75%
    • <70% suggests inadequate O2 delivery
    • >75% may suggest sepsis or inability to extract O2

Central Venous Pressure (CVP)

  • Not reliable for predicting fluid responsiveness
  • CVP <8 suggests hypovolemia (but poor sensitivity)
  • Trends more useful than absolute values

Passive Leg Raise (PLR) Test

  • Auto-transfuse ~300 mL from lower extremities
  • Increase in CO/BP by >10% predicts fluid responsiveness
  • More reliable than static measurements

Fluid Resuscitation

Crystalloids

  • Normal saline (0.9% NS): Risk of hyperchloremic acidosis
  • Lactated Ringer's (LR): Balanced, contains potassium
  • Plasmalyte: Balanced alternative

SMART SALT-ED trials: Balanced crystalloids reduced mortality/AKI vs. saline

Recommendation: Use balanced crystalloids as first-line

Colloids

  • Albumin: Consider in cirrhosis with SBP, hepatorenal syndrome
  • SAFE trial: No difference vs. saline except harm in TBI
  • ALBIOS trial: No benefit in septic shock
  • Starches/gelatins: Associated with AKI, avoid

Vasopressors & Inotropes

Norepinephrine (First-Line)

  • Alpha-1 (vasoconstriction) >> beta-1 (inotropy)
  • Dose: 0.05-3 mcg/kg/min
  • Increases MAP with minimal effect on HR

Vasopressin

  • V1 receptor agonist ‚Üí vasoconstriction
  • Dose: 0.03-0.04 units/min (fixed dose)
  • VASST trial: Add to norepinephrine in septic shock
  • May reduce norepi requirements, no mortality benefit

Epinephrine

  • Alpha + beta agonist
  • Dose: 0.05-2 mcg/kg/min
  • Use: Anaphylaxis, refractory shock, cardiac arrest
  • Concerns: Tachycardia, arrhythmias, splanchnic ischemia, lactic acidosis

Dobutamine (Inotrope)

  • Beta-1 >> beta-2 agonist
  • Increases contractility and CO
  • Dose: 2.5-20 mcg/kg/min
  • Use: Cardiogenic shock with reduced CO
  • Concerns: Tachycardia, arrhythmias, hypotension

Milrinone (Inotrope)

  • Phosphodiesterase-3 inhibitor
  • Increases contractility, reduces afterload
  • Dose: 0.25-0.75 mcg/kg/min
  • Use: Cardiogenic shock, especially with beta-blocker use
  • Concerns: Long half-life, hypotension

MAP Goals

Standard Target: MAP ‚â•65 mmHg

SEPSISPAM trial: No benefit to MAP 80-85 vs. 65-70 mmHg

Exception: Chronic hypertension ‚Üí may need higher MAP (75-85)

Individualize based on end-organ perfusion (UOP, mentation, lactate clearance)

Sepsis & Septic Shock

‚ñº

Definitions (Sepsis-3)

Sepsis: Life-threatening organ dysfunction due to dysregulated host response to infection

  • Organ dysfunction: SOFA score increase ‚â•2 points

Septic Shock: Sepsis + persistent hypotension requiring vasopressors (MAP ‚â•65) + lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation

qSOFA (Quick SOFA - screening tool, NOT diagnostic)

  • RR ‚â•22
  • Altered mentation
  • SBP ‚â§100
  • ‚â•2 points ‚Üí higher risk of poor outcomes

Surviving Sepsis Campaign Guidelines (2021)

Hour-1 Bundle

  1. Measure lactate (remeasure if >2 mmol/L)
  2. Obtain blood cultures before antibiotics
  3. Administer broad-spectrum antibiotics
  4. Begin rapid fluid resuscitation (30 mL/kg crystalloid for hypotension/lactate ‚â•4)
  5. Administer vasopressors if hypotensive during/after fluid resuscitation (target MAP ‚â•65)

Initial Resuscitation

Fluid Resuscitation

  • 30 mL/kg crystalloid within 3 hours for hypotension or lactate ‚â•4
  • Use balanced crystalloids (SMART/SALT-ED trials)
  • Reassess after each bolus

Vasopressor Initiation

  • Don't delay for "adequate" fluid resuscitation
  • Can start during fluid administration if severely hypotensive
  • First-line: Norepinephrine
  • Add vasopressin (0.03-0.04 units/min) if needs escalating norepi
  • Add epinephrine for refractory shock
  • Consider hydrocortisone (200 mg/day) if refractory to fluids + vasopressors

Antibiotic Management

Timing

  • Within 1 hour of sepsis recognition
  • Each hour delay associated with increased mortality

Common Regimens by Source

Community-acquired pneumonia (CAP):

  • Ceftriaxone + azithromycin OR fluoroquinolone
  • If MRSA risk: Add vancomycin

Hospital-acquired/ventilator-associated pneumonia (HAP/VAP):

  • Anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam (pip-tazo, cefepime, meropenem) + vancomycin or linezolid
  • Consider double pseudomonal coverage if high resistance

Complicated UTI/Urosepsis:

  • Ceftriaxone OR fluoroquinolone
  • If ESBL risk: Carbapenem (meropenem, ertapenem)

Intra-abdominal:

  • Pip-tazo OR ceftriaxone + metronidazole
  • If high resistance: Meropenem + vancomycin

Neutropenic fever:

  • Anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam + vancomycin (if catheter-associated or skin/soft tissue)

De-escalation

  • Narrow based on cultures by 48-72 hours
  • Consider shorter courses (7-8 days) if source controlled and improving

Source Control

  • Identify and control infection source within 6-12 hours
  • Examples: Drain abscess, debride necrotic tissue, remove infected devices
  • Surgical consultation if indicated

Corticosteroids

ADRENAL & APROCCHSS Trials (2018):

  • Hydrocortisone did NOT reduce 90-day mortality
  • May reduce time on vasopressors
  • Small increase in hyperglycemia, hypernatremia

Recommendation: Consider hydrocortisone 200 mg/day (50 mg q6h or 100 mg q12h) if refractory shock despite fluids + vasopressors. Continue for ~5-7 days, then taper.

Additional Considerations

Lactate Clearance

  • Target ‚â•10% decrease in first 2 hours (not absolute normalization)
  • But don't "chase" lactate at expense of over-resuscitation

Blood Products

  • Transfuse RBCs if Hgb <7 g/dL (unless active hemorrhage or coronary ischemia)
  • Platelet transfusion for active bleeding or procedures if <50,000
  • No routine FFP unless coagulopathy with bleeding/procedures

Neurological Critical Care

‚ñº

Altered Mental Status

Differential Diagnosis (Mnemonic: AEIOU TIPS)

  • A: Alcohol, Acidosis
  • E: Encephalopathy (hepatic, uremic, hypoxic), Electrolytes, Endocrine
  • I: Infection (meningitis, encephalitis, sepsis)
  • O: Overdose, Opioids
  • U: Uremia
  • T: Trauma, Temperature
  • I: Insulin (hypo/hyperglycemia)
  • P: Psychosis, Poisoning
  • S: Stroke, Seizure, Syncope, Shock

Workup

  • Glucose (bedside)
  • BMP, calcium, magnesium, phosphate
  • CBC, liver function tests
  • Ammonia (if hepatic encephalopathy suspected)
  • ABG (hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis)
  • Toxicology screen, acetaminophen, salicylate levels
  • Blood cultures, urinalysis
  • CT head (if focal findings, trauma, concern for bleed/mass)
  • LP (if concern for meningitis/encephalitis)
  • EEG (if concern for non-convulsive status epilepticus)

Delirium

CAM-ICU (Confusion Assessment Method for ICU)

  • Feature 1: Acute change or fluctuating course
  • Feature 2: Inattention
  • Feature 3: Altered level of consciousness
  • Feature 4: Disorganized thinking
  • Delirium present if Features 1 + 2 + (3 or 4)

Types

  • Hyperactive: Agitation, restlessness
  • Hypoactive: Lethargy, decreased responsiveness (more common, often missed)
  • Mixed

Prevention & Management (Most Important)

  • ABCDEF Bundle (see Sedation section)
  • Reorient frequently (calendar, clock, family)
  • Minimize sedation, avoid benzodiazepines
  • Early mobilization
  • Hearing aids/glasses
  • Sleep hygiene (reduce nighttime disruptions)
  • Treat underlying causes

Pharmacologic Treatment (only if safety concern)

  • Haloperidol 0.5-2 mg IV/PO
  • Quetiapine 25-50 mg PO
  • Not recommended routinely for prevention

Post-Cardiac Arrest Care

Targeted Temperature Management (TTM)

  • Target: 32-36¬∞C for at least 24 hours
  • TTM-1 trial: 33¬∞C vs. 36¬∞C ‚Üí no difference
  • TTM-2 trial: 33¬∞C vs. normothermia (avoid fever) ‚Üí no difference
  • Recommendation: Maintain normothermia, avoid hyperthermia (>37.5¬∞C)

Avoid Hypotension

  • Maintain MAP ‚â•65 mmHg (consider higher if pre-arrest hypertension)

Neuroprognostication

  • Wait ‚â•72 hours after ROSC (longer if cooled or sedated)
  • Multimodal assessment:
    • Exam: Pupillary/corneal reflexes, motor response
    • EEG: Continuous background, reactivity
    • SSEP: Bilateral cortical N20 responses
    • Imaging: Brain MRI (DWI)
    • Biomarkers: NSE, S100B (adjunctive)

Elevated Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

Normal ICP: 5-15 mmHg
Treatment threshold: >20-22 mmHg

Clinical Signs

  • Cushing's triad: Hypertension, bradycardia, irregular respirations
  • Headache, vomiting, papilledema
  • Altered mental status ‚Üí coma
  • Pupillary changes, posturing

Tier 1 Interventions

  • Head of bed elevation to 30¬∞
  • Sedation/analgesia (reduce metabolic demand)
  • Maintain normothermia (treat fever)
  • Maintain euvolemia, avoid hypotension (CPP = MAP - ICP; target CPP >60 mmHg)
  • Maintain normal PaCO2 (35-40 mmHg)
  • Treat seizures

Tier 2 Interventions (if ICP remains elevated)

  • Osmotic therapy:
    • Mannitol 0.25-1 g/kg IV (check serum osm, avoid if >320 mOsm/kg)
    • Hypertonic saline 3% or 23.4% (target Na 145-155)
  • Brief hyperventilation (PaCO2 30-35) as temporizing measure
  • Neuromuscular blockade

Tier 3 Interventions (refractory)

  • Barbiturate coma (pentobarbital; requires continuous EEG)
  • Decompressive craniectomy
  • Hypothermia (33-35¬∞C)

Status Epilepticus

Definition: Continuous seizure >5 minutes OR recurrent seizures without return to baseline

Management Algorithm

0-5 minutes:

  • Assess ABCs, supplemental O2
  • Check glucose, correct if hypoglycemic
  • Obtain IV access

5-20 minutes:

  • Benzodiazepine:
    • Lorazepam 0.1 mg/kg IV (4 mg, may repeat once)
    • Diazepam 0.15 mg/kg IV (10 mg)
    • Midazolam 10 mg IM (if no IV access)

20-40 minutes (Established Status):

  • Second-line AED:
    • Levetiracetam 60 mg/kg IV (max 4500 mg) OR
    • Fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV (max 1500 mg PE) OR
    • Valproic acid 40 mg/kg IV (max 3000 mg)
  • ESETT trial: All three equally effective

>40 minutes (Refractory Status):

  • Continuous infusion + intubation:
    • Midazolam 0.2 mg/kg bolus, then 0.1-2 mg/kg/hr OR
    • Propofol 1-2 mg/kg bolus, then 2-10 mg/kg/hr OR
    • Pentobarbital 5-15 mg/kg bolus, then 0.5-5 mg/kg/hr
  • Continuous EEG monitoring
  • Consult neurology

Renal & Acid-Base

‚ñº

Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

KDIGO Criteria (any of the following)

  • SCr increase ‚â•0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours OR
  • SCr increase ‚â•1.5x baseline within 7 days OR
  • UOP <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours

Stages

  • Stage 1: SCr 1.5-1.9x baseline or ‚Üë‚â•0.3 mg/dL; UOP <0.5 mL/kg/hr √ó 6-12 hr
  • Stage 2: SCr 2.0-2.9x baseline; UOP <0.5 mL/kg/hr √ó ‚â•12 hr
  • Stage 3: SCr ‚â•3x baseline or ‚â•4 mg/dL or initiation of RRT; UOP <0.3 mL/kg/hr √ó ‚â•24 hr or anuria √ó 12 hr

Etiology

  • Prerenal: Hypovolemia, hypotension, reduced effective arterial volume
  • Intrinsic: ATN (ischemic/nephrotoxic), AIN, glomerulonephritis, vascular
  • Postrenal: Obstruction

Workup

  • Urinalysis: WBCs, RBCs, casts, protein
  • Urine electrolytes: FENa, FEUrea
    • FENa <1% suggests prerenal (if not on diuretics)
    • FENa >2% suggests ATN
    • FEUrea <35% suggests prerenal (more reliable if on diuretics)
  • Renal ultrasound: Assess for obstruction, kidney size

Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

Indications (AEIOU)

  • A: Acidosis (severe metabolic acidosis, pH <7.1)
  • E: Electrolytes (hyperkalemia refractory to medical management)
  • I: Intoxication (dialyzable toxins: lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates)
  • O: Overload (volume overload, refractory pulmonary edema)
  • U: Uremia (symptomatic: encephalopathy, pericarditis, bleeding)

Modalities

Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD):

  • Duration: 3-4 hours
  • Pros: Rapid correction of electrolytes/volume
  • Cons: Hemodynamic instability, risk of hypotension
  • Use: Stable patients, hyperkalemia, toxin removal

Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT):

  • Duration: 24 hours continuous
  • Pros: Hemodynamic stability, gradual fluid removal
  • Cons: Requires anticoagulation, immobility
  • Use: Hemodynamically unstable patients, cerebral edema

STARRT-AKI Trial (2020):

  • Accelerated (early) vs. standard (delayed) RRT initiation
  • No mortality benefit with early initiation
  • More patients in standard group recovered without RRT

Acid-Base Disorders

Systematic Approach

  1. Check pH: Acidemia (<7.35) or alkalemia (>7.45)?
  2. Determine primary disorder: Respiratory (PaCO2) or metabolic (HCO3)?
  3. Calculate anion gap (if metabolic acidosis)
  4. Check for compensation
  5. Identify additional disorders (delta-delta)
Anion Gap
AG = Na - (Cl + HCO3)
Normal: 10-12 mEq/L
Adjust for albumin: AG increases ~2.5 for each 1 g/dL decrease in albumin

High Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis (MUDPILES)

  • M: Methanol
  • U: Uremia
  • D: DKA
  • P: Propylene glycol, Paracetamol (late)
  • I: Iron, Isoniazid
  • L: Lactic acidosis
  • E: Ethylene glycol
  • S: Salicylates

Non-Anion Gap (Hyperchloremic) Metabolic Acidosis

  • GI losses: Diarrhea, fistula
  • Renal: RTA, early AKI
  • Iatrogenic: Saline infusion, TPN
Compensation Formulas

Metabolic Acidosis:
Expected PaCO2 = 1.5 √ó (HCO3) + 8 ¬± 2 (Winter's formula)

Metabolic Alkalosis:
Expected PaCO2 = 0.7 √ó (HCO3) + 20 ¬± 5

Respiratory Acidosis:
Acute: HCO3 ‚Üë by 1 for every 10 ‚Üë in PaCO2
Chronic: HCO3 ‚Üë by 3.5 for every 10 ‚Üë in PaCO2

Respiratory Alkalosis:
Acute: HCO3 ‚Üì by 2 for every 10 ‚Üì in PaCO2
Chronic: HCO3 ‚Üì by 5 for every 10 ‚Üì in PaCO2

Electrolyte Emergencies

Hyperkalemia

ECG Changes (progressive):

  • Peaked T waves
  • Widened QRS
  • Prolonged PR
  • Loss of P wave
  • Sine wave ‚Üí VF/asystole

Treatment:

  1. Stabilize cardiac membrane (if ECG changes):
    • Calcium gluconate 1-2 g IV over 2-5 min
  2. Shift K+ intracellularly:
    • Insulin 10 units IV + dextrose 25 g (D50 50 mL)
    • Albuterol 10-20 mg nebulized
    • Sodium bicarbonate 50 mEq IV (if acidotic)
  3. Remove K+ from body:
    • Loop diuretics (furosemide 40-80 mg IV) if UOP adequate
    • Patiromer 8.4 g or Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate 10 g PO
    • Hemodialysis (most effective, for severe/refractory)

Severe Hyponatremia

  • Symptoms: Nausea, headache, confusion, seizures, coma
  • Treatment:
    • 3% NaCl 100 mL bolus, repeat PRN
    • Target: Increase Na by 4-6 mEq/L in first few hours
    • Caution: Avoid correcting >8-10 mEq/L in 24 hours (risk of osmotic demyelination)
Na Increase Formula:
Na increase = (Infusate Na - Serum Na) / (TBW + 1)
TBW = 0.6 √ó weight (men), 0.5 √ó weight (women)

Severe Hypernatremia

  • Correct slowly: ‚â§10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours
  • Use D5W or 0.45% NaCl
  • Caution: Rapid correction ‚Üí cerebral edema
Free Water Deficit:
Free water deficit = TBW √ó [(Na/140) - 1]

Sedation, Analgesia & Delirium

‚ñº

ABCDEF Bundle (PAD Guidelines)

  • A: Assess, prevent, and manage pain
  • B: Both spontaneous awakening trials (SAT) and spontaneous breathing trials (SBT)
  • C: Choice of analgesia and sedation
  • D: Delirium - assess, prevent, and manage
  • E: Early mobility and exercise
  • F: Family engagement and empowerment

Pain Assessment

Scales

  • CPOT (Critical-Care Pain Observation Tool): For intubated/non-communicative
  • BPS (Behavioral Pain Scale)
  • Numeric Rating Scale (0-10) for communicative patients

First-Line Analgesics

  • Fentanyl: 25-100 mcg IV q30min-1hr PRN; infusion 25-200 mcg/hr
  • Hydromorphone: 0.2-0.6 mg IV q1-2hr PRN
  • Morphine: Less preferred in ICU (histamine release, active metabolites in renal failure)

Non-Opioid Adjuncts

  • Acetaminophen 650-1000 mg PO/IV q6h
  • Gabapentin, pregabalin for neuropathic pain
  • Avoid NSAIDs (risk of AKI, bleeding)

Sedation

RASS Scale (Richmond Agitation-Sedation Scale)

  • +4: Combative
  • +3: Very agitated
  • +2: Agitated
  • +1: Restless
  • 0: Alert and calm
  • -1: Drowsy
  • -2: Light sedation
  • -3: Moderate sedation
  • -4: Deep sedation
  • -5: Unarousable

Target: RASS 0 to -2 (light sedation) for most patients

Sedative Agents

Propofol:

  • Dose: 5-50 mcg/kg/min
  • Pros: Rapid on/off, short context-sensitive half-life
  • Cons: Hypotension, propofol infusion syndrome, hypertriglyceridemia
  • Use: Short-term sedation, neuro patients (lowers ICP)

Dexmedetomidine:

  • Dose: 0.2-1.5 mcg/kg/hr (no bolus)
  • Pros: Less respiratory depression, easier communication, less delirium
  • Cons: Bradycardia, hypotension, expensive
  • Use: Weaning sedation, extubated but agitated, ARDS
  • MENDS/SEDCOM trials: Reduced delirium vs. benzodiazepines

Benzodiazepines (Midazolam, Lorazepam):

  • Cons: Delirium, prolonged awakening, propylene glycol toxicity
  • Avoid as first-line per PAD guidelines
  • Use: Alcohol withdrawal, seizures, neuromuscular blockade

Daily Sedation Interruption (SAT)

  • Turn off sedation daily until patient awakens
  • Assess readiness for extubation
  • Resume at half dose if needed
  • Reduces duration of mechanical ventilation, ICU length of stay

Delirium Management

Screening: CAM-ICU or ICDSC at least daily

Prevention (Most Important)

  • Minimize sedation (target RASS 0 to -1)
  • Avoid benzodiazepines
  • Treat pain adequately
  • Early mobilization
  • Sleep hygiene
  • Reorient frequently
  • Sensory aids (glasses, hearing aids)

Treatment

  • Identify and treat underlying cause
  • Non-pharmacologic measures (above)
  • Pharmacologic (only if safety threat):
    • Haloperidol 0.5-2 mg IV/PO q4-6h PRN
    • Quetiapine 25-50 mg PO BID
  • Do NOT use for prevention

Critical Care Procedures

‚ñº

Central Venous Catheter (CVC)

Indications

  • Vasopressor administration
  • Inadequate peripheral access
  • Monitoring (CVP, ScvO2)
  • Hemodialysis
  • TPN administration

Sites

  • Internal jugular (IJ): Preferred (lower pneumothorax risk)
  • Subclavian: Higher success, lower infection, but higher pneumothorax risk
  • Femoral: Higher infection risk, avoid if ambulating

Technique

  • Ultrasound guidance (reduces complications)
  • Maximal sterile barrier precautions
  • Chlorhexidine skin prep
  • Seldinger technique
  • Confirm placement with CXR (tip at cavoatrial junction)
  • Check for pneumothorax

Complications

  • Arterial puncture
  • Pneumothorax/hemothorax
  • Infection (CLABSI)
  • Thrombosis
  • Air embolism
  • Arrhythmia

Arterial Line

Indications

  • Continuous BP monitoring (vasopressor use)
  • Frequent ABGs
  • Hemodynamic instability

Sites

  • Radial (most common): Perform Allen test first
  • Femoral
  • Brachial (higher complication rate)
  • Dorsalis pedis

Endotracheal Intubation

Rapid Sequence Intubation (RSI)

Pre-oxygenation:

  • 100% O2 for 3-5 minutes

Induction Agents:

  • Etomidate 0.3 mg/kg: Hemodynamically stable
  • Ketamine 1-2 mg/kg: Good for asthma, maintains BP
  • Propofol 1-2 mg/kg: Rapid onset, but hypotension
  • Midazolam 0.1-0.3 mg/kg: Slower onset, hemodynamically stable

Neuromuscular Blockade:

  • Succinylcholine 1-1.5 mg/kg: Rapid onset (45 sec), short duration
    • Contraindications: Hyperkalemia, neuromuscular disease, malignant hyperthermia, burns/crush >24 hrs
  • Rocuronium 1-1.2 mg/kg: Onset 60 sec, duration 45-60 min

Confirmation:

  • Chest rise, breath sounds bilateral, EtCO2, CXR

Post-Intubation Hypotension

  • Common! Loss of sympathetic tone + positive pressure ventilation
  • Treat: Fluid bolus, vasopressors

Chest Tube Placement

Indications

  • Pneumothorax (especially tension PTX, hemodynamically significant)
  • Hemothorax
  • Pleural effusion (large, symptomatic, empyema)

Technique

  • Site: 4th-5th intercostal space, mid-axillary line ("safe triangle")
  • Blunt dissection over rib (avoid neurovascular bundle below rib)
  • Direct tube posteriorly and apically
  • Connect to water seal/suction (-20 cm H2O)
  • CXR to confirm

ICU Emergencies

‚ñº

Tension Pneumothorax

Clinical Features

  • Sudden respiratory distress, hypoxia, hypotension
  • Tracheal deviation (away from affected side)
  • Absent breath sounds
  • Distended neck veins
  • Hyperresonance to percussion

Management

  • DO NOT WAIT for imaging
  • Needle decompression: 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line (or 5th ICS, anterior axillary line)
  • Immediate chest tube placement

Massive Hemoptysis

Definition: >200-600 mL blood in 24 hours or life-threatening

Management

  1. Airway protection: Consider early intubation
  2. Position: Bleeding side down (protect non-bleeding lung)
  3. Resuscitate: Large-bore IV, type and cross
  4. Bronchoscopy: Identify source, temporize
  5. Interventional radiology: Bronchial artery embolization
  6. Surgery: If refractory

Acute Upper GI Bleeding

Initial Resuscitation

  • Two large-bore IVs
  • Type and cross
  • Correct coagulopathy: FFP (INR >2), platelets (<50k)
  • Transfusion target: Hgb 7-9 g/dL (TRIGGER trial)

Medications

  • PPI: 80 mg bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion (before endoscopy)
  • Octreotide: 50 mcg bolus, then 50 mcg/hr (if variceal bleed suspected)
  • Erythromycin: 250 mg IV (prokinetic, improves visualization)
  • Antibiotics: Ceftriaxone if cirrhosis

Endoscopy

  • Within 24 hours (12 hours if high risk)
  • Therapies: Clips, cautery, epinephrine injection, banding (varices)

Anaphylaxis

Management

  1. Epinephrine 0.3-0.5 mg IM (1:1000) in anterolateral thigh - FIRST-LINE
    • Repeat q5-15min if needed
    • IV infusion if refractory: 2-10 mcg/min
  2. Remove trigger
  3. Airway management: Prepare for intubation if angioedema/stridor
  4. IV fluids: 1-2 L bolus
  5. Adjunctive:
    • H1 blocker: Diphenhydramine 25-50 mg IV
    • H2 blocker: Ranitidine 50 mg IV
    • Glucocorticoids: Methylprednisolone 125 mg IV
    • Albuterol for bronchospasm
  6. Observe: 4-8 hours minimum

Cardiac Arrest (ACLS)

Immediate Actions

  1. Recognize arrest: Unresponsive, no pulse, apnea/gasping
  2. Call for help, get defibrillator
  3. High-quality CPR:
    • Rate: 100-120/min
    • Depth: ‚â•2 inches (5 cm)
    • Allow full recoil
    • Minimize interruptions (<10 sec)

Shockable Rhythms (VF/pVT)

  • Defibrillate: 200 J biphasic
  • Resume CPR immediately √ó 2 min
  • Epinephrine: 1 mg IV q3-5min (after 2nd shock)
  • Amiodarone: 300 mg IV after 3rd shock (then 150 mg)

Non-Shockable Rhythms (Asystole/PEA)

  • CPR √ó 2 min
  • Epinephrine: 1 mg IV q3-5min (ASAP)
  • Treat reversible causes (H's & T's)

Reversible Causes (H's & T's)

  • H: Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia
  • T: Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade, Toxins, Thrombosis (MI, PE)

Preventive Care & Complications

‚ñº

Venous Thromboembolism (VTE) Prophylaxis

Pharmacologic Prophylaxis

  • Enoxaparin: 40 mg SC daily (30 mg daily if CrCl <30)
  • Heparin: 5000 units SC q8-12h
  • If CrCl <30: Unfractionated heparin preferred
  • Contraindications: Active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia (<50k), high bleeding risk

Mechanical Prophylaxis

  • Sequential compression devices (SCDs): Use if pharmacologic contraindicated
  • Combine with pharmacologic when possible

Stress Ulcer Prophylaxis

Indications (ASHP Guidelines)

  • Mechanical ventilation >48 hours
  • Coagulopathy (platelets <50k, INR >1.5, PTT >2√ó normal)
  • Other risk factors: Burns >35% BSA, hepatic failure, multiple trauma, TBI

Agents

  • Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs): Pantoprazole 40 mg IV/PO daily (preferred)
  • H2 blockers: Famotidine 20 mg IV/PO BID

Catheter-Associated Infections

CLABSI Prevention

  • Maximal sterile barriers during insertion
  • Chlorhexidine skin prep
  • Avoid femoral site if possible
  • Daily review: Remove if not needed
  • Dressing changes per protocol

CAUTI Prevention

  • Remove Foley ASAP (most important)
  • Use only when necessary
  • Sterile insertion technique
  • Maintain closed drainage system

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP) Prevention

VAP Bundle

  • Head of bed elevation (30-45¬∞)
  • Daily sedation interruption + spontaneous breathing trials
  • Oral care with chlorhexidine
  • Peptic ulcer prophylaxis
  • DVT prophylaxis
  • Subglottic suctioning (if available)

ICU-Acquired Weakness

Risk Factors

  • Prolonged mechanical ventilation
  • Sepsis, MODS
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Corticosteroids
  • Neuromuscular blocking agents

Prevention

  • Early mobilization: Out of bed by ICU day 2-3 if able
  • Minimize sedation
  • Glycemic control
  • Physical therapy/occupational therapy consults

Landmark Trials & Key Papers

‚ñº

Respiratory/ARDS

ARMA (NEJM 2000) - Low tidal volume ventilation in ARDS

  • 6 mL/kg IBW vs. 12 mL/kg IBW
  • 22% relative mortality reduction
  • Established lung-protective ventilation

PROSEVA (NEJM 2013) - Prone positioning in severe ARDS

  • 16+ hours/day prone vs. supine
  • 50% relative mortality reduction in PaO2/FiO2 <150

ACURASYS (NEJM 2010) - Neuromuscular blockade in early ARDS

  • 48 hours cisatracurium vs. placebo in PaO2/FiO2 <150
  • Improved 90-day survival (but see ROSE trial)

ROSE (NEJM 2019) - Reevaluation of neuromuscular blockade

  • No benefit when using lighter sedation strategy
  • Current practice: Consider for severe ARDS with difficult oxygenation

FACTT (NEJM 2006) - Conservative vs. liberal fluid strategy in ARDS

  • Conservative strategy improved ventilator-free days
  • No mortality difference

Sepsis

SMART (NEJM 2018) - Balanced crystalloids vs. saline

  • Reduced mortality, AKI, need for RRT
  • Use balanced crystalloids in ICU

VASST (NEJM 2008) - Vasopressin in septic shock

  • No mortality benefit vs. norepinephrine alone
  • May reduce norepinephrine requirements

ADRENAL & APROCCHSS (NEJM 2018) - Hydrocortisone in septic shock

  • No clear 90-day mortality benefit
  • May reduce vasopressor duration

Sedation

MENDS (JAMA 2007), SEDCOM (Lancet 2009) - Dexmedetomidine vs. benzodiazepines

  • Reduced delirium, more days alive without coma/delirium
  • Established dex as preferred agent

Transfusion

TRICC (NEJM 1999) - Restrictive vs. liberal transfusion

  • Hgb 7-9 g/dL vs. 10-12 g/dL
  • Restrictive strategy safe, possibly superior

Post-Cardiac Arrest

TTM-1 (NEJM 2013) - Targeted temperature 33¬∞C vs. 36¬∞C

  • No difference in mortality or neurologic outcomes

TTM-2 (NEJM 2021) - Hypothermia (33¬∞C) vs. normothermia

  • No mortality benefit to hypothermia
  • Avoid hyperthermia (>37.5¬∞C)

Renal

STARRT-AKI (NEJM 2020) - Early vs. standard RRT initiation

  • No benefit to early initiation
  • More patients in standard group recovered without RRT

Ventilator Troubleshooting

High Peak Pressure

  • Check: Biting ETT, secretions, bronchospasm, PTX, mainstem intubation, kinked circuit
  • Differentiate from high plateau pressure (parenchymal issue)

Low Tidal Volumes

  • Check: Cuff leak, circuit disconnect, worsening lung compliance

Hypoxemia

  • Increase FiO2, PEEP
  • Recruitment maneuver
  • Check position (proning)
  • Rule out: PTX, mucus plug, atelectasis, PE

Hypercapnia

  • Increase respiratory rate
  • Increase tidal volume (if plateau pressure safe)
  • Reduce dead space

Goals of Care Discussions

When to Initiate

  • Patient/family request
  • Lack of improvement despite maximal therapy
  • Terminal illness
  • Poor anticipated quality of life

Approach

  • Assess understanding: "What have the doctors told you?"
  • Provide information: Honest, clear, avoid jargon
  • Explore values: "What's most important to your loved one?"
  • Make recommendations: Based on evidence + patient values
  • Summarize and plan: Ensure agreement on next steps

Key Phrases

  • "I wish things were different..."
  • "Given what we know, I'm worried that..."
  • "Would it be okay if I shared what I've seen in similar situations?"
  • Avoid: "There's nothing more we can do" (reframe as focus on comfort)

Final Tips for Success

  1. Be systematic: Use a structured approach for rounds and assessments
  2. Think in terms of organ systems: Neuro, CV, Resp, Renal, etc.
  3. Know your numbers: IBW, tidal volumes, PEEP, MAP goals
  4. Don't delay treatment: Sepsis antibiotics, shock resuscitation
  5. Daily checklist: SBT readiness, line necessity, delirium assessment
  6. Practice procedures: Get supervised experience early
  7. Communicate with family: ICU is stressful; frequent updates help
  8. Take care of yourself: ICU is intense; debrief with team, maintain wellness
